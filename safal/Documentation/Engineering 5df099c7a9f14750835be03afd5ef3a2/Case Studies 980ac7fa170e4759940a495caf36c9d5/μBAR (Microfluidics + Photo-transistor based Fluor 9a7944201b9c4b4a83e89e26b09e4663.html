μBAR (Microfluidics + Photo-transistor based Fluorescence + Electronic Heating)

Device Setup

Equipment Used

  • Degas-driven flow loading based microfluidic cartridge with 96 wells that can be fully loaded in 30 mins after providing sample. The chip needs to be loaded in 2 minutes after opening the vacuum seal (done using a commercial food sealer).
  • Blue excitation LEDs for fluoroscence. Custom made waveguides help to ensure the light is efficiently coupled to the PDMS chip without stray scattering. A monolithic LED driver (MIC3289) supplies a constant current to 3 blue InGaAs LEDs (even as battery voltage decreases).
  • A transparent ITO heater plate with a thermistor to regulate the temperature, and a booster circuit (TPS61085) delivers a constant current to the ITO heater
  • An emission filter below the heater to filter out the fluoroscence to be detected
  • Below the filter is a neoprene foam barrier with holes which optically isolate each phototransistor on the PCB underneath.
  • A chip with 96 phototransistors (one for each well) that detect the floroscence, 3 analog multiplexers (AD732) connected in a 1:32 ratio to the phototransistors, and a transimpedance photoamplifier with input biased to 2.5 V connected to the multiplexers.
  • A custom chip with a ATmega 2560 micro-controller with Arduino bootloader, a GPS receiver, cell phone transceiver, and an SD card.
  • A 4.3" LED touchscreen
  • A 3.7 V, 2000 mAh lithium polymer battery. A third boost converter (LTC3525) delivers a 5V supply to the digital electronics, LCD display, and transimpedance amplifier. A typical assay run lasts approximately 2 hours and consumes 500 mAh.

Key Findings

  • "At its simplest, this instrument would be a handheld heater and LED illuminator which would allow qualitative naked eye readout. "
  • Use of dagas-driven flow for microfluidic propagation
  • Use of waveguide for effective LED illumination
  • Use of foam for prevention of light scatter
  • Cons: The device lacks capability to do sample preparation
